When we read a newspaper or browse a website we are conditioned to quickly scan the headlines and the first few lines of each article. This helps us to grab the essence of that item and decide if we want to read the rest.

On the internet this can be easily done with RSS, which stands for Rich Site Summary or Really Simple Syndication.

When you visit a website, such as The Age, there may be link called Subscribe, Syndicate or even RSS. This has the technical information you need to access the headlines of this site.

Think of this link as having subscription information. You use it to subscribe to the key information of that website such as the headlines and summaries.

But what do we use to access and use these RSS feeds? The answer is an RSS reader, which can be a separate program or part of a modern browser.

A good example of a reader program is RSSowl. If you are interested in subscribing to the RSS feed from a given site, you simply open the feeds link inside RSSowl. One quick way is to cut and paste the link. You have then subscribed to that feed.

For example I have subscribed to The Age Technology Headlines. First stop is the main feed at [www.theage.com.au/rsschannels](http://www.theage.com.au/rsschannels)

Here you will find links for different feeds such as top stories, sport, technology and so on.

I copied the RSS link for Technology and pasted it as a new feed into RSSowl. I have now subscribed to this headline and summary feed.

When I double-click on the feeds name (The Age Technology Headlines), [RSSowl](http://www.rssowl.org) goes out and retrieves the headlines for that site. In some cases, it's not just the headline, but the first few lines of the story.

If you see a summary or headline of interest you can open the item or story. This is usually a webpage, in our case it's an article on Technology section of The Age website. RSSowl is good here. It has a mini-browser built it or you can choose to launch own browser that takes you direct to the story of interest.

The cool [Firefox](http://www.mozilla.org/products/firefox) browser has RSS support built in and refers to them as live bookmarks. Bookmarks are what Internet Explorer calls favourites.

When visiting a page that has RSS feeds available, a Firefox user would see a new icon appear in the bottom of their browser saying there are live bookmarks available on this webpage.

They could choose to add this to their bookmarks. Instead of adding only the site itself, it adds the RSS feed. When they opened that bookmark, or favourite, Firefox goes out and retrieves the headlines so that the headlines appear in the bookmarks menu.

The user can then select that headline and go to the story itself.

Keeping up with news online - especially from papers overseas - has never been easier.
