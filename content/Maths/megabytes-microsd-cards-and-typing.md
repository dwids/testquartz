We take for granted how much real world data our storage devices hold. A quick example: 

One byte of storage is `1 typed character` on the screen/page. (see [[#2026 Updated]])

- 1 **kilo**byte (1000 bytes). A typed A4 page is about 2 KB chars.
- 1 **mega**byte (1 million bytes, MB) is 1 million characters; approx 500 typed pages 
- 1 **giga**byte (GB) is 1,000 megabytes:  about 500,000 typed pages
- 1 **tera**byte is 1,000 gigabytes: about 500,000,000 typed pages (500 million)

Note how we jump by x1000 

My phone's microSD card is tiny (11mm x 15mm) and yet holds 32 GB.  That is 16 million typed pages. 

The poor old Encyclopedia Britannica was about 33,000 printed pages.  So the little phone memory card holds about **484** Britannica's worth of data.

## 2026 Updated
If you are writing in basic English (called ASCII) and not using emojis, you are still using '1 byte per character'. However...

The actual current standard now is called **UTF-8**. It uses:

- 1 byte for basic ASCII (A-Z, 0-9, etc.)
- 2 bytes for most European/Middle Eastern scripts
- 3 bytes for most Asian characters
- 4 bytes for emoji and rare symbols

So "hello" = 5 bytes, but "hello 👋" = 9 bytes (4 for the emoji).
